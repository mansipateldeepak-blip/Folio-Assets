Executive Snapshot

TL;DR
The Company
The Ecosystem We Operate In

Global Industrial Engineering Leader

A globally recognized industrial equipment manufacturer powering large-scale material handling and logistics operations. For decades, the brand has been known for reliability, lean operations, and manufacturing precision. Its equipment supports warehouse networks, distribution centers, and industrial facilities worldwide - environments where uptime, safety, and operational efficiency directly impact revenue.

In recent years, the company has expanded beyond hardware into connected digital ecosystems, investing heavily in telematics, operational visibility, and data-driven optimization to support modern logistics infrastructure.

The Project
System and Product Overview

Enterprise Telematics & Alert Infrastructure

The organization operates an enterprise telematics platform used to monitor and manage fleets of connected industrial equipment across distributed warehouse sites. The system collects real-time operational data from 70,000+ connected assets, helping teams track usage patterns, safety events, maintenance signals, and performance metrics.

The platform supports a multi-layer ecosystem:

Manufacturer Dealer Branch Company Warehouse

To support daily operations, site administrators can configure SMS alert notifications triggered by event-based conditions and threshold-based limits. Each site configures alerts differently - choosing alert type, frequency, and recipients based on their operational needs.

Flexibility was built in. Central visibility was not.

The Challenge
Where the System Was Breaking Down

Decentralized Alerts, Exponential Cost

Across global sites, high volumes of SMS alerts were being triggered with:

  • NO Centralized Deviation Tracking
  • NO Cost Accountability
  • NO Governance Controls

At scale, even a rupee per SMS compounds rapidly when multiplied across thousands of triggers daily. And, the system was invisible.

The Solution
The Strategic Intervention

An insight-driven governance dashboard

I designed a centralized governance layer that:

  • Surfaced deviation spikes by day and time
  • Highlighted outlier sites, alert types, and contributors
  • Applied 80/20 prioritization logic
  • Enabled progressive throttling

The platform shifted from passive reporting to decision-support governance. It started behaving like a consultant - not just showing data, but revealing where action was needed.

The Impact
What Changed - Measurably

Controlled Deviation, Strategic Leverage

  • Directional reduction of unnecessary SMS volume by 64%
  • Improved operational visibility across global sites
  • Prevented recurring alert spikes

It is also the strategic foundation for future subscription-based alert monetization.

From distributed configuration to structured governance.

Chapter 1

Business Context & Operational Reality

The Waters We Are Sailing In
System at Scale
The System & Feature at Scale

The telematics platform functions as an information and control layer for distributed industrial forklift operations. Daily users include: Fleet managers, Operations supervisors, Safety leads, and Entity-wise administrators.

In this ecosystem, complexity is structural - not accidental. Multiple entity layers + regional autonomy + varied operational models create a system designed for flexibility.

And flexibility at enterprise scale introduces management overhead.

Business Risk
The "But Why Now?" and Business Risk

Alert configurations had been scaling steadily across sites - but without centralized oversight. As new regions onboarded and usage increased, SMS alert volumes began rising disproportionately to operational need.

There was no single view to answer:

  • Which sites were over-configured?
  • Which alert types were driving volume?
  • Where deviation was spiking unexpectedly?

Here's why this mattered: Unmonitored alert growth meant escalating SMS costs, increased noise and alert fatigue for users, and reduced responsiveness to genuine safety notifications.

If left unmanaged, the system risked becoming both expensive and ignored.

Constraints
The Anchors

This wasn't a blank canvas. The system was already live, widely used, and deeply embedded in operations.

Live Global System

Alerts were already configured across regions. The solution had to layer on top of what existed.

Multi-variable Alert Logic

SMS volume was influenced by frequency x assets x recipients x alert types - all interacting together.

Site-level Control

Each warehouse managed its own alert settings. Governance needed to guide behavior, not enforce it.

Sprint-based Delivery Reality

The solution had to be incremental, modular, and shippable within existing sprint capacity.

Chapter 2

Research & Strategic Insights

Understanding the Currents Before Steering
Research Game Plan

Before designing anything, we needed clarity. Alert volume was increasing - but we didn't yet know why.

We approached this using a Google Design Sprint mindset:

Understand
→
Map
→
Validate
AI in the Research Process
  • Used a custom Gemini setup to scan 500-700 page documentation across multiple entity levels - extracting all SMS-related logic and patterns in a structured way
  • Analyzed production data shared by engineering using the same setup - identifying patterns and gaps faster than manual analysis
  • Summarized large inputs into clear, decision-ready themes - making stakeholder discussions more focused and decision-driven

Why this approach?

  • The issue was systemic, not interface-level
  • We needed behavioral patterns, not opinions
  • Speed mattered - sprint cycles didn't allow long discovery phases

Why not heavy user research?

  • The problem wasn't usability friction
  • It was configuration logic + governance visibility

We prioritized signal over volume.

Key Findings

Alert volumes were rising disproportionately across certain sites and alert types. And it showed up in the numbers.

Validated User Pain

  1. Overwhelming alert volume with unclear priority
  2. Difficulty identifying which alerts actually required action
  3. Low confidence in configuration impact across sites

Validated Business Pain

  1. Increasing SMS infrastructure costs
  2. Escalating noise in safety workflows
  3. No mechanism to detect deviation spikes
"So many SMS, nothing feels urgent." - "I don't know which alerts are truly critical anymore."

In some cases, manual alert reviews were taking 3-4 hours daily per operations manager. This wasn't just inefficient. It wasn't scalable.

Sailboat Exercise

To align stakeholders, we ran a sailboat framing exercise.

The Aha Moment
The Plot Twist

We assumed rising SMS volume was driven by overall fleet growth. But the data showed something different.

The Aha Moment
A small percentage of sites and alert types were driving a disproportionate share of message deviation.
It wasn't scale alone. It was configuration concentration.

This pattern became clear after analyzing large datasets with AI support, which helped surface concentration areas quickly.

And this changed the strategy from "optimize everything" to "prioritize the vital few."

Problem Statement
Final Problem Statement
How Might We
Provide centralized visibility into alert deviation patterns, so the business can control messaging costs and reduce alert noise - without removing site-level flexibility?

Chapter 3

Ideation, Iteration & Decisions

Finding the Right Way Before Steering
Exploration Paths

Multiple Directions Were Explored

Once we understood how large the alert problem had become, the key question was simple: Where should the fix actually happen?

AI in Ideation
  • Used AI to quickly research different directions, validate early ideas, and identify when approaches were becoming too complex to pursue within current system constraints
✕ Rejected

Option 1: Fix It at the Warehouse Level

Improving tools available to site administrators - implementing local dashboards and configuration controls where warehouse operators could monitor alert volumes and reduce unnecessary notifications.

Why it fell short

Warehouse admins were focused on operations and safety alerts, not SMS cost impact. Since SMS costs were absorbed centrally, there was no strong incentive to configure alerts cautiously. Even well-intended configurations could still generate huge volumes of alerts.

◐ Selected - Incomplete

Option 2: Governance at Manufacturer Level

Centralized governance - a global dashboard providing visibility into alert generation across the entire network. Allow operational leaders to monitor volumes, detect anomalies, and understand patterns.

What worked

This approach solved the visibility problem and enabled strategic oversight.

But there was a gap

While leaders could identify spikes, the system would not prevent excessive notifications from being triggered. Alert storms could still occur before intervention.

✔ The Resolution

Governance + System-Level Throttling (Safety Net)

The team ultimately selected Option 2, recognizing the need for centralized governance and cross-network visibility. However, to address the remaining risk, an additional system layer was introduced.

Example rule: If a system sends more than ~250 SMS within 5 minutes, the platform temporarily blocks additional messages. This was intentionally set well above normal behavior (typical systems generate maximum 100-150 alerts in 5 minutes) - so throttling would only trigger during extreme spikes. Blocked alerts could still be released gradually as the queue cleared.

This throttle is currently being implemented at the site level.

Design Decisions
Three Core Decisions That Shaped the Dashboard
01
Prioritize deviation, not raw volume

Showing total alerts alone wasn't useful. A large warehouse could naturally generate more alerts. The dashboard focused on deviation spikes, sudden behavior changes, and unusual patterns.

This made problems instantly visible.

02
Surface outliers, not just trends

Operations teams needed to quickly answer: "Where exactly is the problem?" The system highlighted outlier sites, outlier alert types, and outlier time patterns.

This turned the dashboard into a prioritization tool, not just a report.

03
Apply 80/20 governance logic

Most alert spikes were driven by a small subset of configurations. Used pareto chart emphasis on top contributors, highest deviation alerts, and sites driving most SMS volume.

This allowed faster operational decisions.

Ideas Not Built
Guardrails We Chose Not to Build
Not Built

No Real-Time Alert Streams

A live alert feed was considered. But it would quickly turn into noise, making it harder to identify patterns. For governance, trends matter more than live activity.

Not Built

No Manufacturer Control Over Site Configurations

Allowing manufacturer admins to directly modify site alert settings was considered and rejected. Warehouse teams needed to retain operational control. The dashboard focuses on surfacing insights, not taking over configurations.

System Reality

During early exploration, I also experimented with a deep drill-down analysis canvas - allowing users to click into a chart, open a large investigation workspace, add multiple charts and filters, and explore alert behavior in depth.

However: We realized the current telemetry system wasn't mature enough yet to support this level of interaction. Instead, we shipped a simpler first version with contextual tooltips and insights. The deeper investigation canvas remains part of the future roadmap.

Enterprise Complexity
Happy Path vs Edge Cases

Happy Path

  1. System detects a spike in SMS alerts.
  2. Administrators review High SMS Volume dashboard to identify affected sites.
  3. SMS Utilization report reveals the source of the alert spike.
  4. Throttling controls are configured to prevent excessive alert generation.

Edge Cases Considered

Error StatesClear messaging when alert data cannot be retrieved or services fail temporarily.
Empty StatesDashboard communicates system health while guiding users on normal behavior.
Slow NetworkProgress indicators ensure administrators know when data is still processing.
Site Data GapsInterface highlights the absence instead of silently failing.

Reflection

What We Did Well

  • Ambitious early exploration helped surface a clearer roadmap for future product evolution.
  • Direct access to manufacturer-level admins provided valuable operational context.
  • Iterative product head input gradually shaped the direction toward an achievable MVP.

What I Would Do Differently

  • Engage with leadership earlier and more frequently during the exploration phase.
  • Ask deeper product questions early to align sooner on MVP vs future roadmap.
  • Clarify technical feasibility earlier when exploring complex system ideas.

What This Project Taught Me

Complex systems rarely become clear overnight. This project made one thing clear - thoughtful design can gradually turn complexity into visibility, control, and confidence for the people who rely on it every day.

It also helped me realize the kind of problems I enjoy solving most. I'm particularly drawn to complex enterprise systems where design brings clarity to real-world operations and decision-making rather than micro-animation heavy, purely visual interfaces.
